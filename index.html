Feaster

Over the past 8 months, I have been working with Feaster, a digital marketing platform that provides users with custom deals and offers on local food and drink via Facebook Messenger. Through Feaster I have had the opportunity to fully create and execute a visual identity, marketing materials, a landing page, and more for a startup that I am very excited and honored to be an integral component of.

Technologies: Adobe Illustrator, Sketch

Please note that all materials here are copyright and property of Feaster, Inc. Please visit our landing page for more information.

Logo Iterations

My first task as a member of the Feaster team was to develop a logo and logo text to fuel all of our branding identity. My goal was to communicate to the user the key features that Feaster delivers — that is, an optimal way to receive personalized deals on local food and drink while saving money.

I started off by brainstorming with some rather cliche illustrations iconic of eating and savings, as well as lettering many iterations of the word "Feaster."

A sample of the brainstorming process behind the Feaster logo and logo text.

After some more brainstorming, I came up with the idea of a piggy bank. Pigs are iconic of eating, and piggy banks are iconic of saving. I decided to add a fork and spoon on the surface of the piggy bank to empahsize the use of this bank as a place for storing the savings a user would garner by using Feaster.

The piggy bank also went through an iterative process. I started off by forming the structure of the pig out of overlapping circles, and then moved on to refine the overall shape and details of the shading.

A behind-the-scenes look at how I created the structure of the piggy bank in Feaster's logo.

Since this was the first time that I had the repsonsbility of creating such a unique logo that was to be used extensively for the branding of a company, I completely underestimated the amount of itertaing I would embark upon after the basic structure of the logo was implemented. Even though I have always been a chronic perfectionist, I even began to get on my own nerves as I hyperfocused on every detail of the logo. But the amount of time I spent making sure every stroke was perfect was more than worth it. It was extremely rewarding to watch something progress from a rudimentary to a much more refined state, and I now feel much more knowledgable about what it takes to implement something as crucial as a brand's logo.

A rough timeline of the logo's progression.

Some of the iterations of the logo text.

After many, many, many iterations, I am very proud to present the final products!

WeatherOrNot

This project is a Rails webapp called WeatherOrNot, an applictaion that utilizes user feedback forms to create personalized suggestions about what to wear based on the weather of a given day. The inspiration behind the app is that, rather often, the same weather conditions feel quite different for different people. WeatherOrNot is a way for users to always know how to dress prepared without having to take any extra time out of their morning rotinues.

Technologies: Ruby on Rails, Sass, Twitter Bootstrap

This past semester I took a course about Ruby on Rails. The course covered many of the integral features and powers of the "Rails magic," and I loved it so much that I have started working on developing some side projects on my own using the Rails framework. (In fact, I have gotten so used to firing up the Rails server that I initially developed this site in Rails and then converted it to pure HTML/CSS/JS to put up on GitHub pages.) Rails is the first backend framework that I worked with, and it has definitely inspired me to continue exploring backend development.

WeatherOrNot began as my final group project for the Rails course mentioned above. After the semester completed, I forked our project repo and continued to work on the app on my own. As of now, it is still a work in progres. A few of the features I have implemented and am in the process of implementing are:

  • AJAX calls to a Weather API in order to gather realtime weather stats (Previously, I seeded the database with random weather instances. The user can select if they want to see real data or random data to play around with more diverse weather conditions.)
  • Integrated Bootstrap to add to the styling I previously implemented with vanilla CSS.
  • Improved the UI of the daily feedback forms.
  • Added a calendar which can be used to access and edit feedback forms from previous days.

Please be sure to check back soon to see the final version of the app once I put it up on Heroku!

Personal Site Iteration

I for some reason decided that it would be a good idea to build up a personal site from scratch — no Bootstrap templates, no frameworks, just a blank HTML document with equally blank stylesheets and scripts. Although I am aware that all of the resources for developers out there render this approach rather impratical, I am very glad that I did it. I've never been more confident in my understanding of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, and it was a lot of fun and extremely rewarding to be able to StackOverflow my way through every obstacle that I encountered and end up with a product that is completely my own!

Note: for now, please fullscreen your browser, since I am in the process of optimizing the responsiveness of the homepage!

Technologies: HTML, CSS, JavaScript, Sass

During my first semester of college, I took a course called The Web Design Decal . It was in this class that I was first exposed to the basics of web development. I fell in love immediately. I have been wanting to code up a personal site ever since, and I am very happy to share the final result!

The Process

My ultimate goal with this site was to create something that fits with my style as both a designer and developer. This site isn't just a place to showcase my projects, but also one of my projects itself.

I started off by defining my color palette, settig up a layout, and choosing fonts. I also made some fun icons that pop up throughtout the site, adding an element of interactiveness to make the site more than just a static portfolio.

The layout I started out with was a very basic one. I had a standard navbar dividing the site up into a few different categories, and clicking on each link led to a different page. After implementing this layout, I got pretty bored of looking at it. I wanted instead something that was a bit more fluid, a bit more interactive, and a lot more fun. So I decide to reimplement the layout into a one page site

Currently, all of the content is "stored" either off to the side or at the bottom of the viewport. I implemented page transitions using a combination of CSS and jQuery animations. A menu is also "stored" to the left of the viewport, which a visitor to my site can use to navigate from section to section.

Conclusion

I had a lot of fun implementing this site. I feel so much more confident in not only my web devleopment abilities, but also my problem-solving abilities in both coding and learning in general.

I'm happy to say that I was able to create a product that feels very "me," and I'm now even more exited to keep learning and developing.
